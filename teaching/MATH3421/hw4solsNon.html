Formative problems 4 solutions (to the ‘not for submission’ Qs)

  1. (a) From chapter 1, the joint posterior is \[\begin{aligned} \pi(\mu,\tau|\boldsymbol{x}) &\propto \pi(\mu,\tau)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu,\tau)\\ &\propto \pi(\mu)\pi(\tau)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu,\tau)\\ &\propto \tau^{g+\frac{n}{2}-1}\exp\left\{-\frac{n\tau}{2}\left[s^2+ (\bar{x}-\mu)^2\right] -\frac{c}{2}\left(\mu-b\right)^2 - h\tau \right\}\end{aligned}\] where \[\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\quad\text{and}\quad s^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar{x})^2.\] Hence the FCD for \(\tau\) is \[\begin{aligned} \pi(\tau|\mu,\boldsymbol{x})&\propto \tau^{g+\frac{n}{2}-1}\exp\left\{-\frac{n\tau}{2}\left[s^2+ (\bar{x}-\mu)^2\right] - h\tau \right\}, \quad \tau>0\end{aligned}\] which we recognise as the kernel of a \(\textrm{Gamma}\left( g+\frac{n}{2}, h+\frac{n}{2}\left[ s^2+(\bar{x}-\mu)^2 \right] \right)\) random variable.

    The FCD for \(\mu\) is \[\begin{aligned} \pi(\mu|\tau,\boldsymbol{x})&\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{n\tau}{2}\left(\bar{x}-\mu\right)^2 -\frac{c}{2}\left(\mu-b\right)^2 \right\},\quad -\infty<\mu<\infty\\ &\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\mu^2[n\tau+c]-2\mu[n\tau\bar{x}+bc] \right) \right\},\quad -\infty<\mu<\infty\\ &\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{n\tau+c}{2}\left(\mu-\frac{n\tau\bar{x}+bc}{n\tau+c} \right)^2 \right\},\quad -\infty<\mu<\infty\end{aligned}\] which we recognise as the kernel of a \(N\left( \frac{bc+n\tau\bar{x}}{c+n\tau}, \frac{1}{c+n\tau} \right)\) random variable.

    (b)

    (i) For random variables \(X\) and \(Y=g(X)\), recall the “usual” formula: \[f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\left|\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)\right|.\] Now let \(\pi_{\psi}(\psi)\) denote the prior density of \(\psi=\log\tau=g(\tau)\). Applying the above formula with \(g^{-1}(\psi)=e^{\psi}\) gives \[\pi_{\psi}(\psi)=\pi(e^{\psi})\times e^{\psi}, \quad \psi\in\mathbb{R}.\] Explicitly, and using the form of the \(\textrm{Gamma}\) pdf, we obtain \[\pi_{\psi}(\psi)= \frac{h^g \exp\{(g-1)\psi-h\exp(\psi)\}}{\Gamma(g)}\exp(\psi),\quad \psi\in\mathbb{R}.\] Given the independent prior specification for \(\psi\) and \(\mu\), the joint prior density is \[\pi(\mu,\psi)=\pi_{\psi}(\psi)\pi(\mu)\] with \(\pi_{\psi}(\psi)\) as above and \[\pi(\mu)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi} \sigma}\exp\left\{-\frac{c}{2}\left(\mu-b\right)^2 \right\}, \quad \mu\in\mathbb{R}.\]

    (ii) The acceptance probability is \[\begin{aligned} \alpha(\boldsymbol{\theta}^*|\boldsymbol{\theta})&=\min\left\{1, \frac{\pi_{\mu,\psi}(\mu^*,\psi^*)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu^*,e^{\psi^*})}{\pi_{\mu,\psi}(\mu,\psi)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu,e^{\psi})} \right\}\\ &=\min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\mu^*)\pi_{\psi}(\psi^*)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu^*,e^{\psi^*})}{\pi(\mu)\pi_{\psi}(\psi)f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu,e^{\psi})} \right\}\\ &=\min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\mu^*)\pi(e^{\psi^*})f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu^*,e^{\psi^*})}{\pi(\mu)\pi(e^{\psi})f(\boldsymbol{x}|\mu,e^{\psi})} \times \frac{e^{\psi^*}}{e^{\psi}} \right\}.\end{aligned}\] Hence, we have written the acceptance probability for a move from \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) to \(\boldsymbol{\theta}^*\) in terms of the prior under \(\mu\) and \(\tau\), and the the likelihood function of \(\mu\) and \(\tau\), with the latter requiring simple substitution of \(\exp(\psi)\) for \(\tau\), and the former using the answer from (b)(i).

  2. (a) We have that \[\begin{aligned} p(\boldsymbol{\phi}|\boldsymbol{\theta})&= \int_{\mathcal{X}} p(\boldsymbol{\phi},\boldsymbol{\xi}|\boldsymbol{\theta})d\boldsymbol{\xi}\\ &=\int_{\mathcal{X}} p_1(\boldsymbol{\xi}|\boldsymbol{\theta})p_2(\boldsymbol{\phi}|\boldsymbol{\xi})d\boldsymbol{\xi}.\end{aligned}\]

    (b) Now, \[\begin{aligned} \int_{\mathcal{X}} p(\boldsymbol{\phi}|\boldsymbol{\theta})\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})d\boldsymbol{\theta} &= \int_{\mathcal{X}}\int_{\mathcal{X}} p_1(\boldsymbol{\xi}|\boldsymbol{\theta})\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})d\boldsymbol{\theta}p_2(\boldsymbol{\phi}|\boldsymbol{\xi})d\boldsymbol{\xi}\\ &=\int_{\mathcal{X}} \pi(\boldsymbol{\xi})p_2(\boldsymbol{\phi}|\boldsymbol{\xi})d\boldsymbol{\xi}\\ &=\pi(\boldsymbol{\phi})\end{aligned}\] as required.

    Aside: There is a practical aspect to this question. Namely, that if we start with two (or more) Markov chain transition kernels \(p_1\), \(p_2, \ldots,\) that admit \(\pi(\cdot)\) as a stationary density, it is possible to form a new transition kernel that is a composition of \(p_1\), \(p_2, \ldots,\). This new transition kernel still admits \(\pi(\cdot)\) as a stationary density. Can you see how this relates to the blockwise/componentwise Metropolis-Hastings algorithm of Chapter 3?
