Formative problems 1 solutions

  1. (a) The likelihood is \[\begin{aligned} \ell(\theta|\boldsymbol{y}) &= \prod_{i=1}^n f_{2}(y_i|\theta)\\ &= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{\theta^{y_{i}}}{y_i !}\exp\{-\theta\}\\ &\propto \theta^{n\bar{y}}\exp\{n\theta\}\end{aligned}\] where \(\bar{y}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n y_i\). The prior density is, for \(\theta>0\), \[\pi(\theta)\propto \theta^{a-1}\exp\{-b\theta\}\] Hence the posterior density is \[\pi(\theta|\boldsymbol{y})\propto \theta^{a+n\bar{y}-1}\exp\{-\theta(b+n)\}\] and we therefore recognise that \[\theta|\boldsymbol{y}\sim Gamma(a+n\bar{y}\,,\,b+n).\]

    (b) The likelihood is \[\begin{aligned} \ell(\theta|\boldsymbol{x}) &= \prod_{i=1}^m f_{1}(x_i|\theta)\\ &= \prod_{i=1}^m \theta\exp\{-\theta x_i\}\\ &\propto \theta^{m}\exp\{-\theta m\bar{x}\}\end{aligned}\] where \(\bar{x}=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m x_i\). Hence the posterior density is \[\begin{aligned} \pi(\theta|\boldsymbol{x}) &\propto \theta^{a-1}\exp\{-b\theta\} \times \theta^{m}\exp\{-\theta m\bar{x}\}\\ &\propto \theta^{a+m-1}\exp\{-\theta (b+m\bar{x})\}\end{aligned}\] and we therefore recognise that \[\theta|\boldsymbol{x}\sim Gamma(a+m\,,\,b+m\bar{x}).\]

    (c) Note that \(n\bar{y}=\sum_{i=1}^n y_i = m\) (total number of emissions) and \(m\bar{x}=\sum_{i=1}^m x_i = n\) (the final time).

    (d) The posterior mode is \[\begin{aligned} m(\theta|\boldsymbol{y}) &= \frac{a+n\bar{y}-1}{b+n} \\ &= \frac{b}{b+n}\frac{a-1}{b}+\frac{n}{b+n}\bar{y}\\ &=\rho \frac{a-1}{b}+(1-\rho)\bar{y}\end{aligned}\] where \(\rho=(b/n)/(b/n +1)\).

  2. We have that \[\begin{aligned} f(\boldsymbol{x})&=\int \pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x})d\boldsymbol{\theta}\\ &=\mathbb{E}[\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x})]\end{aligned}\] where the expectation is wrt \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\sim \pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\), the prior. Hence, given iid samples \(\boldsymbol{\theta}_1,\ldots,\boldsymbol{\theta}_N\) from \(\pi(\cdot)\), we can construct the estimator \[\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i|\boldsymbol{x}).\]

  3. The target is \[\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x})\propto \pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x}).\] The proposal pdf is the prior density \(\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\). Hence, given iid samples \(\boldsymbol{\theta}_1,\ldots,\boldsymbol{\theta}_N\) from \(\pi(\cdot)\), we construct the (un-normalised) weights \[{w}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i|\boldsymbol{x}) \, , \, i=1,\ldots N \, .\] Then, sampling \(N\) times with replacement from \(\{\boldsymbol{\theta}_1,\ldots,\boldsymbol{\theta}_N\}\) using the normalised weights as associated probabilities, gives a sample of size \(N\) approximately distributed according to \(\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x})\). Note that the average un-normalised weight is \[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N w(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}_i|\boldsymbol{x})\] gives a Monte Carlo estimator of the normalising constant \(f(\boldsymbol{x})=\int \pi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\boldsymbol{x})d\boldsymbol{\theta}\).

    Comment on the subleties of the above: when giving the form of the un-normalised weight in the weighted resampling algorithm above, we need a form that we can actually evaluate. Including the intractable normalising constant in the form of the un-normalised weight doesnâ€™t make sense in terms of the practical implementation of the algorithm (unless it is made clear that only the normalised weights are used, for which the intractable constant cancels).

  4. Integrate both sides of the detailed balance equation wrt \(x\) to give \[\begin{aligned} \int \pi(x)p(y|x)dx &=\int\pi(y)p(x|y)dx\\ &= \pi(y) \int p(x|y)dx\\ &=\pi(y)\end{aligned}\] as required for \(\pi(\cdot)\) to be stationary.

  5. (a) Following the hint, we have that (in equilibrium) \[\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}(X_{n+1})&=\alpha \mathbb{E}(X_n)\\ &= \mathbb{E}(X_n).\end{aligned}\] Hence, we must have \(\mathbb{E}(X_{n+1})=\mathbb{E}(X_{n})=0\). Similarly, \[\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Var}(X_{n+1})&= \alpha^2 \mathrm{Var}(X_n)+\sigma^2\\ &= \mathrm{Var}(X_n).\end{aligned}\] Hence, \[\mathrm{Var}(X_{n+1})=\mathrm{Var}(X_n)=\frac{\sigma^2}{1-\alpha^2}.\]

    (b) By assumption, the stationary density is \[\pi(x)=\frac{1-\alpha^2}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp\left\{-\frac{1-\alpha^2}{2\sigma^2}x^2 \right\}.\] We know from Example 1.6.1 that \[p(y|x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{y-\alpha x}{\sigma} \right)^2 \right\}.\] Therefore, upon ignoring terms not involving \(x\) and \(y\) gives \[\begin{aligned} \pi(x)p(y|x)&\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{1-\alpha^2}{2\sigma^2}x^2 \right\}\times \exp\left\{ -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{y-\alpha x}{\sigma} \right)^2 \right\}\\ &\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\left(x^2-\alpha^2 x^2 +y^2+\alpha^2 x^2 -2\alpha xy \right)\right\}\\ &\propto \exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\left(x^2 + y^2 -2\alpha xy \right)\right\}\end{aligned}\] which is clearly symmetric in \(x\) and \(y\). Hence, detailed balance is satisfied for the density \(\pi(x)\) as given above. We may therefore deduce that \(\pi\) is a stationary density of the AR(1) process with \(|\alpha|<1\).

  6. (a) Recall that if \(Z\sim \textrm{Exp}(\lambda)\) then the pdf and cdf are \[f(z)=\lambda e^{-\lambda z}, \quad F(z)=1-e^{-\lambda z}, \quad z>0.\] Now consider the cdf form of the transition kernel: \[\begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}(X_{n+1}\leq y |X_n=x) &=\mathbb{P}(\epsilon_{n+1}\leq y-\alpha x|X_n=x)\\ &=1-\exp\{-\lambda(y-\alpha x)\},\quad y > \alpha x\end{aligned}\] and 0 otherwise. Differentiating wrt \(y\) gives the required transition density as \[p(y|x)=\lambda \exp\{-\lambda (y-\alpha x) \}, \quad y > \alpha x\] and 0 otherwise. We can think of this as the density of a shifted exponential distribution.

    (b) Using \[X_{n+1} = \alpha X_{n}+\epsilon_{n+1}, \quad \epsilon_{n+1}\overset{\textrm{indep}}{\sim}\textrm{Exp}(\lambda)\] recursively, suggests the following algorithm:

    1. Initialise with \(x_0=1\). Set \(n=0\).

    2. Draw \(\epsilon_{n+1}\sim\textrm{Exp}(\lambda)\), e.g. using inverse sampling, and set \(x_{n+1}= \alpha x_{n}+\epsilon_{n+1}\).

    3. Increment \(n\) and go to step 2.

    Note that this is equivalent to drawing \(X_1\) from \(p(\cdot|1)\) and then \(X_2\) from \(p(\cdot|x_2)\) and so on.
